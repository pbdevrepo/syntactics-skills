# Objection Playbook - sync-deal-followup

One playbook per objection type. Apply the matching one when the client raises a concern.

Always get the client's exact words from the rep before routing. Never assume the objection type from a vague summary.

---

## Price Objection

**Signals:** "This is more than we expected", "Can you reduce the cost?", "We have a smaller budget than this", "We got a cheaper quote from someone else"

**What is really being said:** The value has not been communicated clearly, the scope is bigger than they imagined, or a competitor has undercut on price.

**Never do:** Immediately discount. Discounting without a scope change trains the client that your prices are negotiable on request.

**Playbook:**

Step 1 - Understand the gap. Ask: "When you say it's more than expected - is it a specific number, or is it the overall scale of the project?"

Step 2 - Anchor to value before adjusting scope. Say: "Before we talk about the number - the [outcome from Proposal Cover] is what's driving the cost. The scope is sized to get there. If we reduce the budget, we reduce the scope. Can you tell me what the outcome is worth to your business?"

Step 3 - Offer phasing, not a discount. Say: "One option: we phase this so Phase 1 fits your budget and delivers [core outcome]. Phase 2 adds [deferred modules] once you've seen the ROI. Want me to put together a phased version?"

Step 4 - If they name a competitor's lower quote. Say: "Lower quotes usually mean fewer modules, offshore delivery, or fixed-price risk shifted to you. Happy to walk you through what's in ours line by line so you can compare apples to apples."

Step 5 - If they need a hard number lower. Remove scope explicitly. Say: "To hit [their budget], we'd need to remove [specific module]. That means [specific consequence]. Is that a trade-off you can live with?"

Never reduce price without reducing scope in writing.

## Scope Objection

**Signals:** "Do we need all of this?", "Can we cut some of these modules?", "This feels bigger than what we asked for", "We just need something simple"

**What is really being said:** The client is scope-shocked, did not fully understand what they asked for, or is nervous about committing to the full build.

**Never do:** Cut scope arbitrarily. Every module in the proposal was there for a reason from the requirements.

**Playbook:**

Step 1 - Understand which modules feel excessive. Ask: "Which parts feel like more than you need right now?"

Step 2 - Distinguish must-have from deferrable. Say: "Let me break it down. [Module A] is critical-path - without it, [core workflow] doesn't work. [Module B] was in scope because [reason from discovery], but could be Phase 2 if needed."

Step 3 - Surface the consequence of deferring. Say: "If we defer [Module B], here is what that means for [specific thing the client said in discovery]. Is that acceptable?"

Step 4 - Offer a phased version with the critical-path modules only. Say: "If you want to start lean, I can write a Phase 1 scope that covers [modules A, C, D] and defers [B, E]. You'd have a working system in [timeline], and Phase 2 would be a defined follow-on."

Step 5 - Do not defer modules that are technically depended on. Flag this explicitly: "We can remove [Module X] from scope, but it means [Module Y] can't work as designed. I need to flag that before we agree."

## Timing Objection

**Signals:** "We're not ready yet", "Let's revisit in a few months", "We have too much going on right now", "We need to sort out [internal thing] first"

**What is really being said:** There is an internal blocker, a budget cycle, a competing priority, or cold feet.

**Never do:** Say "no problem, reach out when you're ready." Deals that go dark for 3 months rarely come back at the same temperature.

**Playbook:**

Step 1 - Find out if the timing is fixed or soft. Ask: "When you say a few months - is there a specific internal thing that needs to resolve first, or is it more of a bandwidth issue?"

Step 2 - If there is a specific blocker. Say: "What needs to happen on your side before this can move? If I know the trigger, I can plan around it." Then set a specific date to reconnect around that trigger.

Step 3 - Offer a retainer hold or pre-kickoff activity. Say: "If budget and readiness are there but bandwidth is the issue - we could do a low-commitment kickoff: 2-3 sessions to finalize scope and get discovery docs in order, so when you're ready to build, we're not starting from zero."

Step 4 - Lock in a specific reconnect date. Do not accept "sometime in Q3". Say: "Can we put something in the calendar for [specific date] to check in? By then you'll know if [their internal thing] is resolved."

Step 5 - If the timing is indefinite and they cannot name a trigger. Say: "I understand. If things shift, reach out - I'm happy to reprioritize when the timing is right. I'll follow up in [30/60] days to check in." Then follow up once. If no response, close the file.

## No Response (Ghosted)

**Signals:** Opened the email but has not replied. Was warm on the call but went silent. Stopped responding after one or two messages.

**What is really being said:** They are busy, forwarded it internally and are waiting, have a competing priority, or the deal has stalled at a decision they are avoiding.

**Never do:** Keep sending the same follow-up message. Three identical follow-ups trains the client to ignore you.

**Playbook:**

Step 1 - Use the Day 2 / Day 5 / Day 10 schedule from the SKILL.md follow-up section. Each message has a different angle.

Step 2 - After Day 10, try a different channel. If you have a phone number, call. If you connected on LinkedIn, send a brief message. Try to reach someone else at the company if you know one.

Step 3 - If there is an internal champion who is not the decision-maker. Say: "[Champion name], I know the proposal is with [decision-maker]. Is there anything I can do to make it easier for them to review? Happy to do a quick call with both of you if that helps."

Step 4 - Send one breakup message after Day 14 with no response: "Hi [Name], I don't want to keep following up if the timing isn't right. If you'd like to pick this back up later, I'm happy to reconnect - just let me know. Wishing you well either way."

Step 5 - After the breakup message, stop. Do not follow up again unless they initiate.

## Competitor Named

**Signals:** "We're also talking to [Vendor X]", "We got a quote from [Vendor X] that's lower", "[Vendor X] says they can do it faster"

**What is really being said:** Syntactics is not the only option. The client is comparing.

**Never do:** Badmouth the competitor. Never say "[Vendor X] is terrible." It looks defensive and unprofessional.

**Playbook:**

Step 1 - Find out what specifically they are comparing. Ask: "What stands out about [Vendor X]'s proposal compared to ours - is it price, timeline, or something else?"

Step 2 - Acknowledge the competitor without dismissing them. Say: "[Vendor X] is a known option in this space. What I can tell you is what makes our approach different for this specific project."

Step 3 - Name the specific differentiator relevant to this client. Reference the Why Syntactics section from the Proposal Cover. Do not use generic claims.

Step 4 - Offer a comparison call. Say: "Happy to do a quick 20-minute call where I walk you through our proposal line by line so you can see exactly what is and isn't included."

Step 5 - If the competitor quote is significantly lower. Say: "A large price gap usually means different scope, different risk model, or different delivery approach. I'd love to see what's in their quote - if you can share it, I can give you a direct comparison."
